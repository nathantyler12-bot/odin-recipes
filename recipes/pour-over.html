Pour Over Coffee

Pour over coffee is one of the best ways to enjoy your single origin specialty coffee. Credit: prostooleh on Freepik

Pour over coffee is very similar to that Mr. Coffee your parents and grandparents brewed in (and never cleaned for 3 decades) but you have more control over brew. See, in the crusty Mr. Coffee, you don't get to decide where the water is placed in relation to the coffee. It's just a free-for-all and that means the coffee is extracted unevenly. When coffee isn't extracted evenly, it leads to a lot of bitterness and other undesired flavors. Pour overs give you the control of exactly where the water touches the coffee and for how long.

Equipment (see links at bottom of page for my recs)

  • Pour Over dripper, like a Hario V60 or Kalita Wave
  • Filter that matches your dripper. (Cone for V60, flat bottom for Kalita)
  • Fresh, whole bean coffee.Yeah, the pour over will be a better coffee experience. But if you're dumping pre-ground Foldgers into that filter, no amount of technique or new coffee making apparati will save your taste buds.
  • A burr grinder. Need to grind that fresh, whole bean coffee with something.
  • A kettle, preferably gooseneck, of some sort
  • A scale that measures grams
  • A stopwatch (or phone)

Ingredients

This is more about experimentation than an exact amount of ingredients, but it will always be water and ground coffee. Here's a good starting point:

  • 16g freshly ground coffee
  • 320g boiling water

There are 3 things to remember when dialing in your pour over routine:

  • Ratio of coffee to water: this recipe starts out at 1:16 (1 gram of coffee to 16 grams of water), but different coffees will taste better with a lower or higher ratio
  • Grind size: start out with the midway point on your grinder. If the coffee tastes too sour, make the grind size finer. If it tastes too bitter, coarser.
  • Water temperature: lighter roasted coffees need as close to boiling as you can get, while darker roasted need a lower temperature.

Steps

  1. Heat your water to desired temperature
  2. Grind your coffee
  3. Preheat your brewer. Once the water is up to temperature, put the filter in the dripper and pour a generous amount of water over it. Don't forget to pour that water out of your cup or brewer prior to actually brewing the coffee.
  4. Dump the ground coffee in the filter
  5. Tare your scale so you start at 0g and get your stopwatch ready
  6. Bloom the coffee. Pour about 50-60g of water over the bed of coffee and let it set for 45 seconds
  7. After 45 seconds, pour in a circular motion over the coffee until hitting 128g
  8. Wait 20 seconds, then pour again until hitting 192g
  9. Wait 15 seconds, then pour until hitting 256g
  10. Wait 15 seconds, then pour until hitting 320g
  11. Give the dripper a little swirl and let the coffee draw down. Once all the water is passed through, remove the filter and coffee
  12. Enjoy!
